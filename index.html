The Room You're Not In Is Where Your Deals Are Being Made

Mickey Hardy
Co-Founder & Chairman, Special Situations Group
February 18, 2026

Every founder knows the feeling. You spent months on the pitch. You have the deck, the data, the story. You got into the meeting. And then — nothing. The deal went somewhere else, to someone you've never heard of, for reasons that were never explained.

The official reason is usually something polite. "We went in a different direction." "The timing wasn't right." "We're pausing our investment activity." But founders who have been in this world long enough know the real reason, and it's simpler than any of those: someone else was already in the room.

The Invisible Tournament

Capital allocation — whether it's venture funding, a strategic partnership, or an M&A conversation — rarely works the way first-time founders imagine. It's not a meritocracy where the best pitch wins. It's a tournament, and the first round happens before you ever show up.

In every meaningful deal, there's a shortlist. The shortlist isn't built from cold outreach or deck reviews. It's built from relationships, from the conversations that happened at dinner tables and on the sidelines of conferences, from the people who casually mentioned your name to the right person at exactly the right moment.

"If you're not in the room when the shortlist is made, you're not on the shortlist."

This isn't gatekeeping for its own sake. It's how trust scales. Capital allocators — whether they're VCs, strategics, or family offices — are making bets on judgment, on character, on the intangibles that can't be captured in a spreadsheet. And the fastest way to establish those intangibles is through the vouching of someone they already trust.

What the Room Actually Looks Like

People romanticize "the room" as if it's a literal place — a private dining room on Park Avenue, a yacht at Davos, a back table at a conference. Sometimes it is. But more often, the room is a text thread, a group dinner with five people who all know each other, a phone call between two partners at a fund who are catching up on a Sunday afternoon.

The common thread isn't geography. It's access — and access is a function of relationships built over time, maintained through consistency, and deepened through demonstrated judgment. You don't buy your way in. You earn it, or you find someone who's already there to bring you through the door.

How to Change It

The founders who crack this do a few things differently. First, they stop treating their network as a resource to extract from and start treating it as a community to invest in. The question shifts from "who can help me right now?" to "who can I help right now, knowing that the return is relational, not transactional?"

Second, they find an advisor who is already in the rooms that matter — and they structure that relationship correctly. Not a cursory LinkedIn connection, not a one-time coffee, but an actual ongoing relationship where the advisor has skin in the game and a reason to make introductions that go deep rather than wide.

Third, they become someone worth introducing. This means being known for something specific, being seen as the kind of founder who adds value to the rooms they're in — not just someone looking for something.

The deals that define companies aren't found through warm outbound sequences or pitch competition wins. They're made by people who have spent years building the credibility and the connections to be in the room when it matters. The good news is that it's not too late to start. The bad news is that it takes longer than most founders want to admit — unless you have someone who can walk you through the door.
